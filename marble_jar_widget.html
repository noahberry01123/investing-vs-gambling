What this shows. Each draw is an independent event with replacement, so the jar's odds never change — there is no "due" outcome (the gambler's fallacy). On any single draw or short streak, variance dominates and anything can happen. But as the number of draws grows, the law of large numbers pulls your observed average toward the jar's expected value. Switch to "Average per draw" to watch your path squeeze toward the dashed expected-value line.

Why it matters. A jar with more green than red (positive expected value) is an investing-like system: time and repetition work for you. A jar with more red than green (negative expected value — most casino games and pull tabs) is a gambling-like system: the same law of large numbers steadily converges your results toward a loss. Same math, opposite sign.

Note: outcomes are simulated live using your browser's random-number generator, so each run differs. This is an illustration of probability, not market data.
